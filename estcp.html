Case Study

Wildlife conservation for the US military

The US Department of Defense manages over 26 million acres of land around the world, with many installations spanning hundreds of thousands of acres of untouched wilderness. In order to more responsibly manage these areas, the military works with environmental teams to monitor wildlife populations, protect natural resources, and ensure the continued health of the ecosystem.

Motion sensor camera traps are a key tool for species conservation in these areas. Historically, environmental managers would periodically hike or drive to camera sites to retrieve SD cards loaded with images, which exposes field personnel to safety risks, is time-consuming, and often disturbs local wildlife.

Mission Mule partnered with the US Army Corps of Engineers and the US Navy's Naval Facilities Engineering Systems Command under the DoD's Environmental Security Technology Certification Program (ESTCP) to install autonomous data retrieval hardware on three of these remote sites to better monitor wildlife populations, reduce safety risks, and minimize disturbance of local wildlife.*

The Data Mule UAS autonomously flies to each camera trap and circles overhead as the data is wirelessly downloaded. The UAS then visits nearby sites before returning home loaded with data to be offloaded by the flight crew. This technology allows researchers to regularly retrieve images from camera trap sites in hard-to-reach areas without exposing field personnel to excessive driving or hiking in hostile environments or disturbing wildlife with manual data retrieval.

Using the Data Mule UAS fully autonomous flight capability, field teams were able to fly extended missions and navigate complex terrain up to 12km (7.5mi) from the takeoff site. The Data Mule capability gives field teams the opportunity to regularly retrieve data from field sensors from across the range, significantly improving wildlife monitoring coverage and ensuring the continued health of the ecosystem.

Barry M Goldwater Range

Barry M Goldwater Range (BMGR) encompasses over one million acres of largely untouched desert habitat in the American Southwest. The range is home to thousands of species of plants and animals including the Sonoran Pronghorn, an endangered species that has recovered largely due to the ongoing efforts of range management teams at BMGR.

At BMGR, Mission Mule supported existing efforts to monitor wildlife populations with Data Mule-enabled motion sensor cameras located at artificial watering sites that local wildlife depends on to survive the harsh desert climate.

Point Mugu and San Nicolas Island

Alongside BMGR, Mission Mule installed its technology at two US Navy facilities in Southern California's Pacific Coast, Point Mugu and San Nicolas Island. With their locations on the coast, these installations are known for their diverse wildlife including elephant seals, sea lions, and Brandt's cormorants, a species of concern.

Mission Mule's technology allows field teams to autonomously retrieve images from camera traps located in a tidal marsh at Point Mugu and from within coastal bird colonies on San Nicolas Island without disturbing the birds. The systems allowed field teams continuous access to areas that are difficult to regularly reach on foot for much of the year.

Wrapping Up

At Point Mugu, San Nicolas Island, and BMGR, Mission Mule technology was used to accelerate ongoing conservation efforts of local species and ensure ongoing stewardship of some of the last untouched wildlands in the United States.
